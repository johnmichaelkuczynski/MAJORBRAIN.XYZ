I argue that the pragmatic conception of truth, which equates truth with usefulness, is fundamentally flawed. Pragmatism posits that the truth of a belief or proposition is determined by its practical effects, its usefulness in achieving certain ends. To understand why truth cannot be equated with usefulness, consider the nature of truth-values in classical logic. Classical logic is characterized by explicit rules of inference, step-by-step deduction, binary truth values, context-independence, and compositional semantics [P1]. In this framework, a statement is either true or false, independent of external factors such as who is evaluating the statement or when it is being evaluated. This binary nature of truth-values ensures consistency and objectivity in logical reasoning and argumentation.

In contrast, the pragmatic view ties truth to the notion of usefulness, which can vary significantly across different situations. What is useful in one context may not be useful in another, and what is useful for one individual may not be useful for another. For instance, a belief that leads to a successful outcome in one scenario may lead to failure in another due to changed circumstances or different individual needs. Thus, the variability of usefulness undermines the stability and universality that truth-values are supposed to represent.
